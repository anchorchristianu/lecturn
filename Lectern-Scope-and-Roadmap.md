# Lectern — Scope & Roadmap

*The book-creation workflow, and what Lectern should own as coach and editor.*

## The core decision

Lectern owns one well-defined arc: **the spoken word → a professionally clean,
structurally sound manuscript**, plus a clean handoff to everything after.
That's stages 1–9 of the standard 19-stage publishing workflow. Stages 10–19
(design, typesetting, ISBNs, printing, distribution, audiobook) are a different
craft on different software; Lectern's job there is to *hand off beautifully*,
not to do the work.

## The governing principle: autonomy scales inversely to the editing level

Professional editing runs **developmental → line → copy → proofreading**, applied
strictly in that order (CMOS 18th ed., 2024) — there's no point perfecting prose
in a chapter you'll later cut. AI's competence runs *opposite* to that pyramid:
rock-solid at the mechanical bottom, increasingly shaky toward the judgment-heavy
top.

So Lectern's editorial authority should scale inversely with the level:

| Level | AI competence | Lectern's posture |
|---|---|---|
| Proofreading (mechanical) | High | Just fix it |
| Copyediting (mechanical) | High, voice-risk low | Fix mechanics; flag facts/claims |
| Line editing (style/voice) | Risky — flattens voice | Suggest only; before/after; author approves |
| Developmental (structure/judgment) | Weakest | Surface & ask; editorial letter; defer to humans on high-stakes |

**Why Lectern is well-positioned:** every warning about AI editing is the same
warning — voice flattening — and the industry's fix is editing against a
locked-in voice profile. That voice fingerprint is already Lectern's core
mechanism. The discipline that makes AI editing dangerous for generic tools is
the discipline Lectern was built around.

## Stage-by-stage scope (stages 1–9)

**1–2 · Concept & gathering — own as coach.** Purpose, audience, positioning,
scope, working TOC; gather the author's *own* material. Boundary: no external
research-as-content (fabrication / voice-drift risk). *Status: live (intake +
capture/upload).*

**3 · First draft — own (the marquee).** Draft chapters from captured material,
in the author's voice, with `[GAP:]` markers. *Status: live.*

**4 · Author self-revision — own as coach.** Reorganize, clarify, fill gaps,
strengthen; conversational, author-steered. *Status: live (revise + interview).*

**5–6 · Developmental / substantive — first-pass review, not a replacement.**
A manuscript-wide editorial letter: chapter-order logic, redundancy between
chapters, argument gaps, audience fit, framework realization, promises vs.
delivery, pacing. Position honestly as a sharp first read, not a substitute for a
human developmental editor on a high-stakes book. *Status: building now.*

**7 · Line editing — assist only, maximum caution.** Tracked suggestions with
before/after, against the voice fingerprint, biased toward leaving phrasing
alone. Suggestion-and-approve is the safety mechanism, not a nicety. *Status:
partial (light "polish").*

**8 · Copyediting — own the mechanics, flag the rest.** Grammar, punctuation,
spelling, consistency, capitalization, style guide (Chicago). Build a **style
sheet / consistency tracker** and **claim-flagging** (mark facts, quotes, stats,
and especially scripture/citations for the author to verify — never assert).
*Status: to build.*

**9 · Proofreading (text) — own.** Final mechanical pass on the text. Boundary:
post-layout proofreading (reading the *designed pages*) is out of scope. *Status:
to build.*

## The coaching move that ties it together

**Enforce the order.** Professionals are nearly unanimous: don't copyedit before
the structure is settled. An author who gets stuck — or polishes commas to avoid
a broken chapter — needs a system that says "not yet." Extend the stage rail into
a **journey map for the whole book** that gates the editing levels and gently
refuses premature polishing. This is where Lectern beats a pile of editing tools.

## The handoff line — out of scope as work, smoothed as artifacts

- **Compile / export** to Markdown / DOCX / EPUB, with front & back matter and the
  **style sheet** — the package a designer or Vellum/Atticus needs.
- **Optional Launch Kit:** book description, back-cover copy, keywords,
  categories, author bio, endorsement-request templates. (Marketing copy and
  metadata are the one place past stage 9 where AI is genuinely good and widely
  accepted.) Derived from the finished book, in the author's voice.
- **Everything else** — interior/cover design, typesetting, page-proofing the
  layout, ISBN & distribution, printing, audiobook, and anything touching
  permissions, plagiarism, or legal — Lectern names, points to the right human or
  tool, and stays out of.

## Build order

1. **Developmental Review** — manuscript-wide editorial letter. *(in progress)*
2. **Leveled editing passes** (Line / Copy / Proof) as distinct modes, each with
   the autonomy above and tracked, approvable suggestions.
3. **Style sheet + claim-flagging** — especially valuable for scripture-and-
   citation-heavy books.
4. **Journey map + level gating** — the coaching spine.
5. **Compile / export + handoff package**, then the optional **Launch Kit**.

## The throughline

Lectern is the author's coach for the whole journey and their *first-pass*
professional editor for the mechanical and structural work — doing the ~80% of
editorial labor that's genuinely automatable, protecting the voice the whole way,
and producing a manuscript clean enough that a human editor (if the author wants
one) starts from a strong draft instead of a rough one.
